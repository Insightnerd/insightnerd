Most resume rejections happen before a human ever really reads them — either an applicant tracking system filters them out, or a recruiter skims for ten seconds and moves on. Here are the patterns that quietly kill a resume's chances, and the fast fixes for each.

Red flag 1: Duties instead of outcomes

Problem: "Responsible for maintaining dashboards and reports."
Fix: "Rebuilt the weekly reporting pipeline, cutting turnaround from 3 days to same-day."

A duty tells the reader what you were supposed to do. An outcome tells them what happened because you did it.

Red flag 2: No numbers anywhere

Even an estimate beats nothing. "Improved query performance" says less than "improved query performance by roughly 40%, from a 12-second to a 7-second average runtime." Recruiters and hiring managers are scanning for evidence of impact, and numbers are the fastest signal.

Red flag 3: A skills section that's just a keyword dump

Listing 25 tools with no context reads as padding, not depth. Group by category (Languages, BI Tools, Databases) and only include what you can speak to confidently in an interview — a long list you can't defend under questioning does more harm than good.

Red flag 4: Formatting that breaks parsing systems

Tables, text boxes, columns, and graphics can scramble how an applicant tracking system reads your resume — sometimes dropping entire sections. A single-column, standard-font layout parses reliably almost everywhere.

Red flag 5: A generic objective line

"Seeking a challenging role in a dynamic company" tells the reader nothing. Either cut it, or replace it with a two-line summary that states your specific focus area and your strongest, most relevant win.

The five-minute audit

Before you send your next application, check: does every bullet have a number? Does every bullet describe an outcome, not just a task? Would a parsing tool read this cleanly? Small fixes, but they're often the difference between a callback and silence.
